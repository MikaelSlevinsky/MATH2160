It's usually quite straightforward how to make sense of the vector p-norms:

∥x∥1=∑i=1n|xi|,∥x∥2=∑i=1n|xi|2,∥x∥∞=max1≤i≤n|xi|.

What is perhaps not so obvious are induced matrix norms:

∥A∥p=sup0≠x∈Cn∥Ax∥p∥x∥p=max∥x∥p≤1∥Ax∥p=max∥x∥p=1∥Ax∥p.

To visualize these, we will restrict our attention to m=n=2; that is, vectors live in the plane. For p=1 and p=∞, we get lozenges, and for p=2, we get ellipses.

Similarly, it is important to grasp a strong intuition on p-norms of functions. This is most easily visualized when looking at an approximation error. Suppose we wish to approximation f(x)=ex on [−1,1]. Then a degree-2 polynomial interpolant through the points {−1,0,1} has the form:

p2(x)=e−1x(x−1)2−(x−1)(x+1)+e1x(x+1)2.

Let's plot the integrand in the p-norm:

∥f−p2∥p=(∫−11|f(x)−p2(x)|pdx)1p.
